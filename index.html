Course Learning Goals

This practicum provides hands-on experience designing and developing curricular assets for online learning experiences as part of HGSE's Teaching and Learning Lab. Grounded in research-based design principles, students engage with real projects, develop a professional portfolio, and build practical instructional design skills alongside TLL faculty and staff.

Capstone Project

AI in Learning Design: From Exploration to Practice

Why This Project Matters

T127 is about getting hands-on experience creating learning materials while connecting what research says about learning. I chose AI in learning design because AI opens real opportunities to make learning more meaningful. Some of the challenges that I encountered during the leadership project could be overcome by using AI. When working with diverse groups of adult learners, made clear that learning materials and sessions need to actively construct mental models in learners. I believe most of those challenges could be addressed through thoughtful, AI-informed design. This project is my attempt to explore what that looks like in practice.

Overarching Project Goals
  • Understand AI's role in learning design and what it means for how we create learning experiences
  • Define what AI-fluent practice actually looks like for someone designing learning experiences
  • Know when AI helps for analysis, brainstorming, production versus when human judgment is irreplaceable
  • Conduct a needs analysis and translate findings into concrete learning design requirements
  • Make informed choices on platforms and formats that serve the pedagogical purpose

Learning Theories in the Tamil Nadu School Leadership Programme

"

Good design requires holding theory and context in productive tension.

‚Äî Reflection on the Tamil Nadu School Leadership Programme

The behaviorist system we were working within

The Tamil Nadu school education system operates at a scale of 37,000 public schools and runs largely on behaviorist theory. This programme was mandatory for school leaders, and leaders understood that being seen as cooperative mattered for their reputation and career progression.

As designers we were caught between two different approaches part of the system wanted visible outputs, checklists, and proof of compliance, while the other half wanted spaces for questioning, reinterpreting, and co-designing practice.

Where we tried to bring in constructivist elements

We used cases and dilemmas pulled directly from Tamil Nadu schools, asking leaders to interpret them through their own experience and debate different approaches in small groups. We saw headteachers describing failures, questioning assumptions, and collectively realizing they were spending almost no time in classrooms.

The challenge: many participants still approached the programme as training that tells them what to do. We realized that simply putting people in groups and calling it "collaborative learning" isn't enough. Social constructivism requires intentional design around norms, roles, and psychological safety.

The cognitive load problem

We tried to activate prior knowledge sessions began by asking leaders to map how they spend their time or describe a classroom observation. These helped surface existing mental models and created hooks for new concepts.

Where we struggled was sequencing. Leadership is inherently complex, and for leaders who had never reflected on instructional leadership or used student data beyond exam results, we were asking them to analyze and evaluate before they understood. Looking back, it was jumping cognitive levels too quickly. Lengthy slide decks, overlapping models, and quick shifts between formats made overload worse especially for leaders working in a second language.

Where leaders actually are: The Dreyfus model

Most leaders fell between novice and advanced beginner. Novice leaders rely on rules and directives when asked how they know learning is happening, they pointed to exam pass percentages. Advanced beginners recognized patterns. The competent group thought strategically.

The assumption we made during design was that participants were at least competent, when many were novices. We designed for 'competent' practitioners while working mainly with novices and advanced beginners. Novice leaders filled planning templates with generic phrases for lack of mental models; meanwhile, experienced leaders found parts too basic.

What motivates leaders: Self-Determination Theory

Autonomy was largely absent leaders were nominated without consultation and attendance was mandatory. We created pockets of choice, but the framing of compulsion dominated. Competence was mixed: well-scaffolded modules gave leaders an "I can do this" moment, but overly abstract content left some overwhelmed. Relatedness had real strengths the residential format meant leaders lived and learned together, though hierarchies sometimes limited openness.

But when sessions tapped intrinsic stories leaders who were concerned for marginalized students, proud to be the first graduate in their family became authentic. They discussed making classrooms joyful, supporting struggling learners beyond just pass rates.

Working with cultural scripts

The programme sits within a culture where learning is transmission from authority. "Training" means experts lecturing, participants listening, then being tested. My instincts pushed in another direction I wanted discussion, questions, and learning built together. But openly going against the usual way of teaching would not have been accepted.

So I stayed within the familiar format while gently changing it: short talks followed quickly by cases, using questions to surface ideas first. Deciding when to go along with these expectations and when to push back is still something I am figuring out.

My expertise blind spots

I overestimated leaders' readiness for abstract concepts. Because I was comfortable with "data-driven instruction" or "growth mindset," I assumed presenting these would be enough. I underestimated how much misalignment is possible where basic infrastructure is a daily struggle.

I also underestimated the emotional work required. For a headteacher who built their identity around being a strict administrator, shifting towards collaborative leadership isn't just a technical change. They need space, safety, time and not just frameworks.

Moving forward

Learner variability matters far more than I understood. Designing for an "average" participant means designing for no one. The Dreyfus model shows that differentiation is essential for meeting people where they are.

This reflection has also clarified where I am in my own learning journey. I signed up with decent conceptual understanding but almost no practical wisdom about designing at system scale. The gap between understanding a theory and applying it in a messy, politically charged context is real and I am still learning how to hold theoretical integrity and political reality in large-scale projects.

The Core Tension

Behaviorism and constructivism are not just theoretical opposites they describe real competing pressures in any large-scale programme. Small constructivist moves within a compliance-oriented environment can still create genuine learning: the right case study, a well-structured task, a moment of reflection.

The Design Lesson

A smaller number of core ideas with more worked examples would have supported deeper schema building. Coverage is not the same as learning. The theories we read are not just abstract frameworks they live in every design choice, every facilitation move, and every response to the room.

Week 1

Number System Converter

First Encounter: Making Abstract Number Systems Tangible

The Pedagogical Challenge

Number systems beyond decimal feel abstract and disconnected from real-world computing. How do we help students understand The most fundamental challenge students face is the misconception that fractions and decimals are completely different types of numbers rather than different notations for representing the same quantities?

Learning Objectives

Students will be able to understand:

  • EQUIVALENCE Different fractions can represent the same quantity (equivalent fractions)
  • FRACTION-DECIMAL CONNECTION The same quantity can be represented as a fraction or a decimal
  • VISUAL PROOF Physical models (fraction strips) help us see and prove mathematical relationships
  • MATHEMATICAL PATTERNS Predictable patterns exist in fraction-decimal relationships

Pedagogical Approach

Concrete-to-Abstract Progression: Students start with visual fraction strips (concrete) before moving to symbolic notation (abstract), building conceptual understanding before procedural fluency.

Multiple Representations: The same quantity appears simultaneously as a visual model (fraction strips), symbolic fraction, and decimal number reinforcing that these are different notations for the same mathematical idea.

Discovery Through Interaction: Rather than being told "1/2 = 0.5," students physically manipulate fraction strips to discover equivalent fractions and their decimal representations, making abstract concepts tangible.

Pattern Recognition: Interactive exploration reveals mathematical patterns (e.g., halving denominators, decimal equivalents) that build number sense rather than rote memorization.

The Solution

An interactive fraction visualizer that lets students explore fraction equivalence and fraction-decimal relationships through manipulable fraction strips with instant visual feedback.

Week 5

Integer Matching Space Game

Evolution: Becoming an AI-Educator Partner

The Pedagogical Challenge

Negative numbers are abstract and hard for students to visualize. How do we make the concept of "below zero" concrete and spatially intuitive?

Learning Objectives

Students will be able to:

  • Understand the concept of positive and negative integers on a number line
  • Visualize negative numbers as positions below zero using spatial metaphors
  • Match numerical representations with visual models and written descriptions
  • Apply integer concepts to real-world scenarios (elevation, temperature, depth)
  • Recognize patterns in how integers relate to zero as a reference point

Pedagogical Approach

Concrete-to-Abstract Progression: The space elevator metaphor provides a concrete, relatable model before introducing abstract numerical concepts. Students can "see" negative numbers as physical positions.

Multiple Representations: By matching three different representations (numeral, visual, description), students build flexible mental models and deeper understanding of what negative numbers mean.

Spatial Reasoning: Using vertical space (up/down) leverages students' intuitive understanding of physical height and depth, making abstract mathematics tangible.

Active Discovery: Through gameplay, students discover patterns and relationships rather than being told rules, promoting constructivist learning.

Immediate Feedback: Game mechanics provide instant feedback on matches, allowing students to self-correct and refine their understanding through iteration.

The Solution: Space as Metaphor

"The space theme naturally reinforced the concept of positive and negative integers - with the space station at zero, going up for positive numbers, and down for negative numbers."

Week 6

Prime Numbers Space Shooter

Mastery: Rapid Prototyping Enables Pedagogical Experimentation

The Pedagogical Challenge

Prime numbers feel abstract and intimidating. Students memorize lists without understanding why a number is prime. How do we make prime number identification intuitive through gameplay?

Learning Objectives

Students will be able to:

  • Identify prime numbers accurately and efficiently through repeated practice
  • Distinguish between prime and composite numbers in real-time decision-making
  • Understand why a number is or isn't prime using factorization reasoning
  • Recognize special prime categories (Mersenne primes, twin primes, palindromic primes)
  • Apply divisibility rules and mental math strategies to determine primality
  • Develop fluency and confidence with number properties through gamified practice

Pedagogical Approach

Scaffolded Learning: The hint system provides just-in-time support by showing prime factorization, helping students understand the "why" behind composite numbers rather than just memorizing.

Spaced Practice: Gameplay naturally creates spaced repetition as numbers appear repeatedly across rounds, strengthening long-term retention of prime number patterns.

Gamification for Motivation: Leaderboards, achievements, and progression systems tap into intrinsic motivation, making practice feel like play rather than drill-and-kill.

Adaptive Challenge: Multiple difficulty levels allow students to work within their zone of proximal development, preventing frustration and boredom.

Conceptual Depth: Special prime achievements (Mersenne, twin primes) introduce advanced concepts to high-achieving students, providing extension opportunities within the same platform.

Immediate Feedback Loop: Real-time consequences (score changes, visual feedback) create tight feedback loops that accelerate learning and pattern recognition.

The Transformation

"My previous experience creating interactive websites was honestly pretty complicated... But now? It feels like I can create a small game-based learning platform in just a couple of hours, even if I only have a rough idea to start with. That transformation is what makes it magical to me."

The Game Concept

A math-educational space shooter where players identify and destroy prime numbers while avoiding composites, featuring multiple game modes and difficulty levels.
